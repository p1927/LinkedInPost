# Content Post Types — Patterns & Templates

> A format-agnostic playbook. These patterns work across LinkedIn, Instagram, email, and WhatsApp. Pick the post type that fits your intent, fill in the template, publish.

---

## How to Use This Document

Each section covers one **post type** — what it is, why it works, the structural pattern behind it, and a fill-in template. Some types have multiple template variants for different tones or contexts.

The golden rule across all types: **the hook is everything.** The first line decides whether anyone reads the rest.

## 1. The Informational / News Post

### What it is
You share something that happened — a piece of news, a report, a stat, a development — and add your unique perspective on what it means. You are the filter, not the reporter. The news is the hook; your take is the value.

### Why it works
People are drowning in information but starved for interpretation. When you connect a news item to what it means for your specific audience, you become a trusted signal in a noisy feed. Posts that share new data or industry news receive high save rates because readers bookmark them to reference later.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → The news or stat, stated sharply (1–2 lines)
CONTEXT    → Why this matters / what led to it (2–3 lines)
YOUR TAKE  → What this means for your reader (3–5 lines)
IMPLICATION → What should they do / think differently about?
CTA        → Question or prompt to spark discussion
```

### Template A — Breaking News / New Data
```
[Surprising or significant news headline in your own words].

Here's what's actually going on:

[2–3 sentences of context — what changed, why it happened, 
who it affects]

What this means for [your audience]:

→ [Implication 1]
→ [Implication 2]
→ [Implication 3]

The part most people are missing: [your unique angle or 
contrarian read on the news]

What's your take? Does this change how you're approaching 
[relevant topic]?
```

### Template B — Stat / Report Drop
```
[Shocking or counterintuitive stat].

Source: [Name of report/publication]

Why this number matters:

[2–3 sentences unpacking what the stat reveals about a 
larger trend]

If you're in [industry/role], this is worth paying attention 
to because [specific implication].

I've been saying [related belief] for a while. This data 
confirms it.

What does this change for you?
```

### Template C — Curated News Summary (newsletter-style)
```
[X] things worth knowing in [industry/topic] this week:

1. [News item] — [one sentence on why it matters]
2. [News item] — [one sentence on why it matters]
3. [News item] — [one sentence on why it matters]
4. [News item] — [one sentence on why it matters]
5. [News item] — [one sentence on why it matters]

My read on all of this: [2–3 sentence synthesis — what's 
the bigger pattern here?]

Which of these are you watching most closely?
```

### What makes it land
- Lead with the news, not with "I read an article about..."
- Your interpretation is the product. Anyone can share a link.
- Keep your take tight — one strong angle beats three weak ones.
- End with a question that connects the news to their reality.

## 2. The Week in Review / Recap Post

### What it is
A structured look back at what happened — in your work, your industry, your team, or your own thinking — over the past week. It can be personal (what I did, learned, shipped) or professional (what happened in the industry). The best ones are both.

### Why it works
Recaps are inherently relatable because they mirror how people process their own weeks. They create a rhythm — readers come back each week expecting them. They also perform well because they feel honest and unpolished compared to evergreen content, which makes them feel more human.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → A single highlight, lowlight, or surprising moment
THE RECAP  → 3–5 items from the week (bullets or numbered)
THE META   → What does this week reveal about something bigger?
PERSONAL   → One honest reflection or feeling
NEXT WEEK  → What's coming / what you're watching
CTA        → Invite readers to share their week
```

### Template A — Personal Work Recap
```
This week in [your work / your life / your head]:

✅ [Thing you shipped, completed, or made progress on]
📉 [Something that didn't go as planned — be honest]
💡 [Best idea or insight that came up]
📚 [Something you read, watched, or heard that shifted your thinking]
🙋 [An unexpected conversation or connection]

The theme of this week: [one sentence that captures the mood or lesson]

If I had to do one thing differently: [honest reflection]

What did your week look like? Drop the highlight (or lowlight) below.
```

### Template B — Industry Week in Review
```
Here's what happened in [industry] this week — and what it means:

📌 [Event/news item 1]: [one sentence take]
📌 [Event/news item 2]: [one sentence take]
📌 [Event/news item 3]: [one sentence take]
📌 [Event/news item 4]: [one sentence take]

The bigger picture: [2–3 sentences connecting the dots — 
what's the trend or tension these items all point to?]

What I'm watching next week: [1–2 things]

Anything I missed? What was your headline of the week?
```

### Template C — Project / Team Recap
```
Week [X] of [project/initiative] — here's the honest update:

What we set out to do:
[Brief reminder of the goal]

What actually happened:
→ [Win or milestone]
→ [Challenge or setback]
→ [Unexpected development]

What we're adjusting:
[One key change in approach, thinking, or priority]

What we learned that we didn't expect to:
[The real insight from the week — often the most interesting part]

Building in public is uncomfortable sometimes.
But the accountability is worth it.

What would you have done differently in our position?
```

### What makes it land
- Be specific — "raised $X", "got 47 rejections", "slept 4 hours". Vague recaps die.
- Include at least one lowlight or honest admission. Highlight reels get skipped.
- The weekly cadence is the product. Consistency builds a following for this format faster than almost anything else.

## 3. The Personal Story Post

### What it is
A narrative drawn from your own life or career that contains a lesson your audience can use. Not a diary entry — a story with a point. The story is the vehicle; the insight is the destination.

### Why it works
Personal story posts receive 38% more engagement than purely informational posts. Humans remember stories far better than facts. When you share a vulnerable, specific story, readers see themselves in it — and that recognition is what makes them comment, save, and share.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → The end result or a shocking moment (creates an open loop)
CONTEXT    → Set the scene — where were you, what were you doing?
THE TURN   → The moment everything changed
THE STRUGGLE → What it was actually like (be specific, be honest)
THE INSIGHT → What you learned or how you see it differently now
THE UNIVERSAL → Why this matters for anyone reading this
CTA        → Open question connecting your story to theirs
```

### Template A — The Failure Story
```
[Specific, uncomfortable outcome that shows vulnerability].

[Year / time period]. [Set the scene in 1–2 lines.]

I [what you did / tried / believed].

It didn't work.

[What actually happened — specific, honest, no sugarcoating]

The moment I realised something was wrong: [specific detail]

I spent [time period] doing [wrong thing] before I understood 
that [the real lesson].

What I know now: [The core insight in 1–2 sentences]

If you're in the middle of something similar — [reassurance 
or reframe].

Have you been through something like this? 
What did you do when [specific aspect of the struggle]?
```

### Template B — The Surprising Win
```
[Counterintuitive result — the less obvious outcome, not the brag]

[Context: what you were trying to do, what you expected]

What I actually got instead: [the surprise]

Here's how it happened:

[Step 1 or moment]
[Step 2 or moment]  
[The unexpected turning point]

The lesson I didn't expect: [the insight that emerged from the gap 
between expectation and outcome]

I used to think [old belief].
Now I think [new belief].

What's a time things turned out better (or worse) than you expected?
```

### Template C — The Mentor / Conversation Story
```
[Person's first name] told me something [X] years ago that 
I didn't understand at the time.

"[The quote or advice — make it specific, not generic]"

I smiled. I nodded. I completely ignored it.

[What you did instead and what happened as a result]

[Time period] later: [the moment the advice clicked]

[What changed once you finally understood it]

Who gave you advice you dismissed at the time 
but later turned out to be exactly right?
```

### Template D — The Short Story (under 150 words)
```
[Hook — one startling sentence]

[3–4 sentence story with specific sensory detail]

[The turn — one sentence where things shift]

[The lesson — two sentences max]

I tell this story because [why it matters to your audience].

[Question]
```

### What makes it land
- Start with the end or the peak moment — don't build up to it, open with it.
- Use specific numbers, dates, names, and places. Specificity is what makes a story feel real.
- The story should have a lesson your reader can apply — not just one that reflects well on you.
- Short paragraphs and generous white space. This is not an essay.

## 4. The Event Insight Post

### What it is
You attended something — a conference, a panel, a meeting, a dinner, an industry event — and you're sharing the insight that actually mattered. Not a generic "great event!" post. A real observation drawn from being in the room.

### Why it works
Most event posts are noise ("Thanks for having me at [event]!"). Posts that extract and package a real insight from an event perform far above average because they deliver value to people who weren't there — which is the vast majority of your audience. They also signal real-world activity and authority.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → The single most interesting thing you heard or saw
CONTEXT    → Event name, who was speaking, what the moment was
THE INSIGHT → What was said / done / revealed — specific, not vague
YOUR READ  → What you think it means beyond the obvious
IMPLICATION → What this signals about your industry or topic
CTA        → What does your reader think about this?
```

### Template A — The Single Insight Extract
```
The most interesting thing I heard at [event name] this week:

"[Direct quote or sharp paraphrase from a speaker or conversation]"

— [Speaker name / role, if relevant]

Why this hit differently: [2–3 sentences on your specific reaction 
and why this insight is more significant than it sounds]

The uncomfortable implication: [The thing most people in the room 
probably didn't want to sit with]

[Your own related experience or perspective — 2–3 sentences]

What do you think? Is this directionally right, or is there 
something the speaker is missing?
```

### Template B — The 3-Insight Recap
```
I was at [event / conference / panel] yesterday.

Here are the 3 ideas I'm still thinking about:

1. "[Insight 1 — quoted or paraphrased]"
   Why it matters: [one sentence]

2. "[Insight 2]"
   Why it matters: [one sentence]

3. "[Insight 3]"
   Why it matters: [one sentence]

The through-line across all three: [2–3 sentences connecting them 
into a single bigger idea]

What I'm going to do differently because of it: [specific action]

Were you there? What was your biggest takeaway?
```

### Template C — The Hallway Conversation
```
The best insight at [event] didn't come from the stage.

It came from a conversation with [description, not necessarily name] 
in the hallway during the coffee break.

They said: "[The quote]"

I'd been thinking about [related topic] for a while. 
But I'd been framing it wrong.

[What shifted in your thinking after this conversation]

This is why I keep going to these things even when the agenda 
looks thin. The margin conversations are where the real ideas live.

What's the best thing you've heard at an event recently — 
on stage or off?
```

### Template D — The Contrarian Event Take
```
I just got back from [event name].

Everyone seemed very excited about [the dominant narrative 
or theme of the event].

I came away with a different feeling.

[Your contrarian observation — specific, evidence-based, 
not just contrary for its own sake]

Here's what I noticed that contradicted the official narrative:
→ [Observation 1]
→ [Observation 2]
→ [Observation 3]

I could be wrong. But I don't think so.

Was anyone else there? What was your read?
```

### What makes it land
- Name the event and roughly when it was. Recency signals relevance.
- Quote real things. Paraphrasing is fine, but "someone at a conference said" is weak.
- The hallway conversation format often outperforms the main stage recap because it feels more exclusive.

## 5. The Industry Trend & Commentary Post

### What it is
You identify a pattern — something shifting, emerging, declining, or accelerating in your industry — and share your interpretation of what it means. Different from a news post because you're drawing the insight yourself from observations over time, not reacting to a single news item.

### Why it works
Trend posts position you as someone who sees around corners. They are widely shared because people use them to signal their own industry intelligence — "I saw this coming too" — which is a powerful social currency. They also age well and continue to pick up engagement weeks after posting.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → State the trend in one sharp, surprising sentence
EVIDENCE   → 2–3 data points, examples, or observations that prove it's real
THE SHIFT  → What this is replacing or disrupting (the before/after)
WHY NOW    → Why is this happening at this particular moment?
IMPLICATION → What should your reader do / think / prepare for?
CTA        → Do they agree? What are they seeing?
```

### Template A — The Emerging Trend
```
[Something is changing in your industry]. 

And most people haven't noticed yet.

Here's what I'm seeing:

→ [Observation / data point 1]
→ [Observation / data point 2]
→ [Observation / data point 3]

12 months ago, [what the norm was].
Today, [what's shifting].
12 months from now, I think [prediction].

Why this is happening now: [The underlying driver — 
technology, behaviour, economics, or culture?]

What this means if you're in [relevant role/industry]:
[Specific, actionable implication]

Am I seeing this wrong? What's your read on where this is heading?
```

### Template B — The Dying Trend
```
[Common practice or belief in your industry] is quietly dying.

It's been declining for [timeframe].

Here's the evidence:
1. [Evidence point with specifics]
2. [Evidence point with specifics]
3. [Evidence point with specifics]

What's replacing it: [The new thing or approach]

Why most people are still doing the old thing: 
[Human / organisational / economic reason — be honest and generous]

The inflection point is coming. 
If you wait until it's obvious, you'll be late.

What's one thing in your industry that you think is quietly dying 
but most people haven't admitted it yet?
```

### Template C — The Pattern Recognition Post
```
I've been noticing something.

Three completely unrelated things happened this week:
→ [Example 1]
→ [Example 2]
→ [Example 3]

On the surface, they have nothing to do with each other.

But they're all pointing at the same thing: [the underlying pattern]

What this tells me about [your industry/topic]: 
[2–3 sentences of synthesis]

The question I'm sitting with: [The open, honest tension 
you haven't resolved yet]

What patterns are you seeing right now?
```

### What makes it land
- Be specific about timing: "in the last 6 months" or "since [event]" beats "lately".
- The best trend posts name something before everyone else is talking about it.
- Acknowledge the counter-argument briefly — it makes your take more credible, not weaker.

## 6. The Satirical / Sarcastic Post

### What it is
You critique, mock, or gently skewer something absurd in your industry — a cliché, a behaviour, a piece of advice, a type of person, a trend — using humour, sarcasm, or deadpan observation. Done well, it gets shares because it says what everyone is thinking but nobody is saying out loud.

### Why it works
Humour is one of the most powerful forms of social currency. When a post makes someone laugh or smirk, they immediately want to share it so they look funny and perceptive too. Satirical posts also build personality and distinctiveness — they make you memorable in a feed full of earnest thought leadership.

> **Caution:** Punch at ideas, behaviours, and absurd patterns — not at people. The best satire is generous: it laughs with the reader, not at a target.

### The Pattern
```
SETUP    → Establish the absurd norm or behaviour being satirised
BUILD    → Stack 2–3 examples that escalate the ridiculousness
PUNCHLINE → The subversion or the twist that lands the joke
TRUTH    → The real underlying critique (optional — sometimes 
            the joke is enough)
CTA      → Invite them to add their own examples
```

### Template A — The "Signs You Are..." Format
```
Signs you're in [industry/role]:

→ You've said "[specific jargon phrase]" with a straight face
→ You've attended a meeting to plan the agenda for a future meeting
→ [Absurd specific behaviour]
→ [Another absurd specific behaviour]
→ [One more that lands the hardest — save the best for last]
→ You're nodding along to this list instead of doing your actual job

[Punchline or deadpan closer]

Add yours in the comments.
```

### Template B — The Corporate Translator
```
What they say vs what they mean:

"Let's circle back on this" = I have no intention of ever discussing this again

"We need to be more agile" = We're about to reorganise the entire company for no reason

"This is a great opportunity for growth" = This is going to be miserable

"[Industry-specific phrase]" = [What it actually means]

"[Industry-specific phrase]" = [What it actually means]

"[Industry-specific phrase]" = [What it actually means]

The gap between what gets said and what gets meant in [industry] 
is basically its own dialect.

What would you add to this list?
```

### Template C — The Deadpan Observation
```
[Absurd observation stated completely seriously, as if it's normal]

Anyway, [pivot to something mundane, as if nothing happened]

[The subversion or the second absurd thing that escalates it]

It's [year]. This is where we are.

[Optional: one line of genuine underlying frustration or insight]

Tell me I'm not the only one.
```

### Template D — The Fake Announcement / Parody
```
Exciting news: We're thrilled to announce that [company/team] 
is pivoting to [satirical direction that captures an absurd industry trend].

Effective immediately, all [normal thing] will be replaced by 
[the over-hyped thing everyone is doing].

Our [job title] will now be called [absurdly long title].

We are disrupting [normal thing] with [buzzword-laden description 
that means nothing].

A note from our CEO: "[Corporate speak parody quote]"

We're deeply grateful for the opportunity to [more jargon].

[Company name]: [Satirical tagline]

(Real talk: [One sentence of what you're actually critiquing and 
why it matters])
```

### Template E — The "Hot Take" One-Liner Format
```
[One-line joke or observation]

That's it. That's the post.

(Okay one more: [Second one-liner that escalates the first])
```

### What makes it land
- Specificity makes satire funny. "synergising cross-functional stakeholder ecosystems" lands; "corporate jargon" doesn't.
- The best satirical posts have a real truth underneath. The laugh is the hook; the insight is the value.
- Keep it short. Satire that over-explains kills itself.
- Invite additions in the CTA — the comment section becomes the best part.

## 7. The Appreciation & Recognition Post

### What it is
You publicly acknowledge someone — a colleague, a mentor, a team member, a client, a creator — for something specific they did, said, or created. Or you express genuine gratitude for an experience, a milestone, or a community. The emphasis is on *specific* — vague appreciation posts are ignored; precise, detailed ones are remembered.

### Why it works
Recognition posts perform surprisingly well because they trigger multiple types of engagement simultaneously: the person being recognised shares it, their network engages out of loyalty, and the reader feels inspired or moved. They also reveal your character — how you talk about others when you're not trying to sell anything says a great deal about who you are.

> **The trap to avoid:** Generic appreciation posts ("So grateful to work with such an amazing team!") generate no engagement and signal nothing. Be specific. Name the person. Name the thing. Say why it mattered.

### The Pattern
```
HOOK       → The specific action, moment, or quality you're recognising
WHO        → Who is this person and what do they do
THE MOMENT → The specific thing that happened
WHY IT MATTERS → What it meant to you / what it revealed about them
THE BROADER POINT → What does this example teach about [value or idea]?
CTA        → Invite others to recognise someone similar
```

### Template A — Recognising a Person
```
[Person's name] did something I want to recognise publicly.

[The specific thing they did — not vague, not general. 
The more specific the detail, the more it lands.]

Here's why this matters more than it might seem:

[The context that makes this action notable. 
What would have been easier to do? What did it cost them?]

I've worked with a lot of people who [common behaviour].
[Person's name] consistently [the opposite, better behaviour].

[One sentence on what this says about their character or values]

If you get the chance to [work with / learn from / follow] them — 
take it.

[Person's name], thank you for [the specific thing].

Tag someone whose work deserves more recognition.
```

### Template B — Thanking a Mentor / Teacher
```
[X] years ago, [Person's name] told me something I didn't 
fully appreciate at the time.

"[The specific thing they said or taught you]"

I've been thinking about it again this week because [context].

[How that advice or their example has shown up in your work or life]

[One sentence on what they didn't get credit for at the time]

The people who shape us most are often not the ones 
who get the loudest applause.

Thank you, [Name]. This one mattered.
```

### Template C — Team / Group Recognition
```
Last [week/month/quarter], [team or group] did something remarkable.

[What they did — specific outcome, specific effort, specific context]

What most people didn't see:
→ [The invisible work or sacrifice behind the result]
→ [The challenge they had to navigate]
→ [The decision they made that no one will know about]

Results like this don't happen by accident.
They happen because [the value or quality they exemplified].

[Team/group name]: you did the thing. 
Genuinely proud of what you built here.

Who on your team deserves more recognition right now?
```

### Template D — Appreciation for a Community / Audience
```
[X] [days/weeks/months/years] ago, I started [the thing you started].

I had no idea [honest reflection on your lack of certainty].

Since then: [what happened — specific numbers or moments if you have them]

But the number I keep coming back to isn't [metric].
It's [the human thing — conversations, connections, messages].

Thank you for reading. Thank you for pushing back. 
Thank you for the DMs that said [something real and specific].

[One honest thing you've learned from your community]

This one's for you. What's something you've gotten out of 
this community / feed / newsletter that you didn't expect?
```

### Template E — Appreciation for a Piece of Work / Creation
```
I've been thinking about [piece of work — book, article, talk, 
product, post, film] by [Person/Creator].

Specifically, this line / moment / idea:

"[The specific quote or description of the moment]"

[What it unlocked or shifted in your thinking — be specific]

[Why this kind of work is rare / what it takes to make something 
like this]

If you haven't [read/watched/experienced] it yet — 
[strong, honest recommendation].

[Creator's name or handle] — genuinely, thank you for making this.

What's a piece of work that has stayed with you longer 
than you expected?
```

### What makes it land
- Specificity is non-negotiable. "She always goes above and beyond" = forgettable. "She rewrote the entire deck at midnight because she noticed the framing was off" = memorable.
- The appreciation post is not about you. Minimise "I feel grateful" and maximise "here is what they did and why it matters."
- The broader point at the end (what this teaches about a value or quality) elevates the post from a thank-you note to something worth reading for everyone, not just the person being recognised.

## Hook Formula Library

The first line of every post is the most important line. Use these formulas to find your hook before you write anything else.

### For Informational / News Posts
```
[Surprising stat or fact]. Here's what it actually means.
[Thing everyone thought was true] just got proven wrong.
Something big changed in [industry] last week. Most people missed it.
The [report/study] everyone is citing is missing the most important number.
```

### For Recap / Review Posts
```
This week broke me. (Here's what I learned from it.)
A week in [your world]: the good, the embarrassing, and the useful.
[X] things happened this week that I didn't expect.
Week [number] update: I got this wrong.
```

### For Personal Story Posts
```
I [did something / had something happen] that I'm still 
not sure I should share.
[X] years ago, I [made a specific decision]. 
Here's how it actually played out.
The moment I realised I'd been wrong about [belief] for [timeframe].
I [startling outcome]. Here's the story nobody tells you.
```

### For Event Insight Posts
```
The best thing I heard at [event] wasn't from the stage.
One sentence from [speaker name] at [event] reframed 
how I think about [topic].
I just got back from [event]. Here's my honest take.
Everyone at [event] was talking about [X]. I was thinking about [Y].
```

### For Trend & Commentary Posts
```
Something is quietly dying in [industry].
I've been noticing a pattern. It took me [timeframe] to name it.
[X] is changing faster than most people realise.
The next [timeframe] in [industry] is going to be very different 
from the last. Here's why.
```

### For Satirical Posts
```
[Absurd behaviour stated completely seriously]
Hot take: [thing that is actually the obvious truth, 
stated as if it's controversial]
Signs you work in [industry]:
We're excited to announce that [satirical pivot]
```

### For Appreciation Posts
```
[Person's name] did something I want to say out loud.
I want to tell you about [Person's name].
The person who most changed how I think about [topic] 
isn't someone most people know.
[X] years ago, someone gave me [time/help/advice].
I've never properly thanked them publicly. Until now.
```

## Universal Writing Rules

These apply to every post type, every platform, every tone.

**1. One idea per post.**
If you can't state what your post is about in one sentence, it's two posts. Split it.

**2. Mobile-first formatting.**
Short paragraphs. Maximum 2–3 lines per block. One blank line between blocks. No walls of text.

**3. Say the specific thing.**
Numbers, names, dates, places. "A few months ago" → "In October 2024". "A lot of people" → "47% of respondents". Specificity builds credibility.

**4. Lead with the point, not the background.**
Most posts bury the interesting thing in paragraph three. Cut paragraphs one and two. Start there.

**5. The best CTAs are questions that are easy to answer.**
"What do you think?" gets ignored. "What's one thing you'd add?" or "Has this happened to you?" gets answered.

**6. Write the closing line first.**
Know where you're going before you start. The post is a journey to a destination — know the destination.

**7. Read it out loud before posting.**
If you stumble over a sentence, rewrite it. If it sounds like corporate speak out loud, it reads like corporate speak on screen.

**8. Give before you ask.**
Deliver genuine value — an insight, a laugh, an observation, a story — before you invite any action. Posts that lead with the ask get skipped.
