# Readability Rules

## Overview

Readability is not just grammar — it is the visual and structural experience of text. Posts that are technically correct but visually dense lose readers before the message lands. These rules govern line length, whitespace, emoji usage, and formatting choices.

---

## Line Length Rules

**Optimal Range:** 50-75 characters per line for body text in most contexts

**Why it matters:** Lines that exceed 80 characters require the eye to travel far to find the next line start, increasing cognitive load and skipped lines. Lines under 40 characters create choppy rhythm that feels fragmented.

**Mobile adjustment:** On mobile screens (where most social content is read), 40-60 characters per line is optimal. Platform-native apps auto-wrap, but line break choices still signal intentionality.

**Practical rule:** Write for a line that ends before the reader needs to scroll horizontally. When in doubt, break earlier rather than later.

---

## Whitespace Levels

### Dense
**Whitespace Ratio:** low — minimal blank lines, paragraphs run together
**Best For:** academic or technical documentation, long-form reports, contexts where brevity signals seriousness
**Risk:** Overwhelming to skim; loses casual readers immediately
**Pattern:** 1 line break between paragraphs, 3-5 sentences per paragraph

### Airy
**Whitespace Ratio:** high — frequent blank lines, short paragraphs, generous spacing between ideas
**Best For:** LinkedIn feeds, Instagram captions, WhatsApp messages, content targeting mobile-first readers
**Pattern:** 1-2 sentences per paragraph, double line break between each block, visual pauses built in

### Scannable
**Whitespace Ratio:** medium — structured whitespace with clear visual hierarchy
**Best For:** Email newsletters, Telegram channels, longer educational content
**Pattern:** Bold section headers, bullet lists, short intro paragraph, clear CTA block at end

## Emoji Usage Levels

### None
**Signal:** Formal, authoritative, institutional
**Appropriate For:** Legal, financial, academic, senior executive communication; contexts where emoji would undermine credibility
**Rule:** Zero emoji in body and headline. If a platform supports it, plain text only.

### Minimal
**Signal:** Professional but human; approachable without being casual
**Appropriate For:** LinkedIn thought leadership, professional email, B2B content targeting directors and above
**Rule:** 0-2 emoji per post; used for emphasis or to mark the end of a section, not for decoration; no emoji in the opening line

### Moderate
**Signal:** Engaging, personality-forward, social-native
**Appropriate For:** LinkedIn engagement posts, Instagram educational content, Telegram channels
**Rule:** 3-6 emoji per post; used as visual bullet points or section markers; one emoji per paragraph maximum; consistent with brand voice

### Heavy
**Signal:** Casual, high-energy, community-oriented
**Appropriate For:** WhatsApp broadcast messages, Instagram lifestyle content, consumer brand social
**Rule:** Emoji can appear in most lines; functions as punctuation; should feel natural in the voice, not forced

## Formatting Elements

### bold
Use to: emphasize the single most important phrase per section; draw the eye to key data points; make section-opening statements stand out
Rule: Maximum 2 bold phrases per 200 characters of body text; bolding everything bolds nothing

### bullet-list
Use to: present 3+ parallel items that would form a run-on sentence; scannable tips or steps; comparison content
Rule: Each bullet should be grammatically parallel; 5-7 bullets maximum before splitting into two lists; avoid bullets for narrative content

### numbered-list
Use to: sequential steps where order matters; ranked content (countdown structure)
Rule: Same parallelism rules as bullet lists; use when the sequence is the content

### italic
Use to: titles, foreign phrases, technical terms on first use, light emphasis that doesn't warrant bold
Rule: Use sparingly; more than 3 italic phrases per post creates visual noise

### line-break
Use to: create visual rhythm; separate ideas that are related but distinct; give the reader a micro-pause
Rule: On platforms without formatting support, line breaks are the primary formatting tool; use deliberately

### divider
Use to: separate major sections in longer content (email, long Telegram posts); signal a shift in topic or register
Rule: Use maximum 2 dividers per post; visual separators work best in email and structured content, not conversational platforms

---

## Scanability Tips

1. **Lead with the most important idea** — readers who skim will read the first line of each paragraph; make those lines count
2. **One idea per paragraph** — mixing two ideas in one block confuses skimmers
3. **Use parallel structure in lists** — all items start with a verb, or all start with a noun; never mix
4. **End with a clear action or takeaway** — the last line has outsized retention weight
5. **Test the skim path** — read only the first sentence of each paragraph; if the post still makes sense, it's well-structured

## Readability and Persona Interaction

Persona language signals interact with typography choices:

| Persona Language Style | Recommended Whitespace | Emoji Level | Formatting Elements |
|---|---|---|---|
| formal | dense or scannable | none | bold, numbered-list |
| professional | scannable | minimal | bold, bullet-list |
| casual | airy | moderate | line-break, emoji |
| conversational | airy | moderate to heavy | line-break, emoji |
| technical | scannable | none or minimal | bold, code-block, numbered-list |
