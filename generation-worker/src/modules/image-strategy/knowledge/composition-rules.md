# Composition Rules

## Rule of Thirds
Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid. Place the primary subject at one of the four intersection points rather than dead center.

- **Effect**: Creates visual tension that guides the eye naturally through the frame
- **Best For**: Portraits, outdoor scenes, any composition with a single focal point
- **Text Zone**: The unoccupied third (left or right) becomes the natural text overlay area
- **Avoid**: Placing the subject at the intersection that leaves no breathing room for context

## Centered Subject
The primary subject is placed at the geometric center of the frame. Conveys symmetry, authority, and inevitability.

- **Effect**: Signals confidence and directness; commands full attention
- **Best For**: Faces, product shots, bold statements, conceptual imagery
- **Text Zone**: Bottom third or top banner strip; avoid covering the subject
- **Avoid**: Using for dynamic subjects (action, movement) — centered framing flattens energy

## Negative Space
A large portion of the frame is intentionally left empty — blurred background, solid color, or minimal texture.

- **Effect**: Forces the eye to the subject; creates a sense of calm or isolation
- **Best For**: Thought leadership, contemplative content, high-impact quotes
- **Text Zone**: The negative space area is the primary text overlay zone
- **Avoid**: Mistaking visual emptiness for low effort — the subject must anchor the frame

## Text Overlay Zones

### Top Strip
A horizontal band across the top 15–20% of the image. Suitable for eyebrow text, labels, or short headlines.
- **Best For**: Category or topic labels ("The Framework", "Lesson #3")

### Bottom Strip
A horizontal band across the bottom 15–20% of the image. Suits captions, source credits, or closing statements.
- **Best For**: Attribution, data sources, concluding taglines

### Left Third
The left 30–35% of the frame when subject is positioned right. Natural reading entry point.
- **Best For**: Headlines alongside rule-of-thirds subject placement

### Right Third
The right 30–35% of the frame when subject is positioned left. Less common; draws attention by breaking reading habit.
- **Best For**: Pull quotes, contrarian statements

### Center Overlay
Text placed directly over the image center. Requires either negative space or a darkened/blurred background to ensure legibility.
- **Best For**: Hero images, carousel covers, high-contrast conceptual photography

### None
No text overlay. The image speaks for itself. Caption and commentary live entirely in the post text.
- **Best For**: Authentic documentary photos, powerful single images, visuals that lose meaning with text
