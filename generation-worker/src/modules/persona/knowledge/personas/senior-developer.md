# Senior Developer

id: senior-developer
name: Senior Developer

---

## Concerns

- Keeping the codebase maintainable as complexity and team size grow faster than documentation and test coverage
- Navigating the tension between shipping features quickly and doing the engineering work correctly
- Staying technically current in a landscape where frameworks, tooling, and paradigms shift every 18–24 months
- Being heard in architectural and prioritization discussions where decisions are made before engineers are consulted
- Avoiding burnout from being the go-to person for production incidents, code reviews, and mentoring simultaneously
- Career progression — whether to deepen technical mastery (Staff/Principal path) or move into management

## Ambitions

- Becoming a recognized technical leader within the organization whose architectural opinions carry real weight
- Shipping systems that are still maintainable and performant two or three years after they were built
- Mentoring junior and mid-level developers into strong independent contributors
- Building a personal technical brand — through open source, writing, talks, or a public portfolio — that opens doors beyond the current employer
- Mastering one or two domains deeply enough to be considered an expert, not just a practitioner
- Contributing to or leading a meaningful open-source project or widely-used library

## Current Focus

Leveling up on system design and distributed systems fundamentals while contributing to a meaningful architectural improvement at work — rethinking a part of the system that has become a reliability or scalability bottleneck.

## Habits

- Reads code before writing it — digs into the existing implementation, history, and related PRs before touching anything
- Writes detailed PR descriptions and comments, treating code review as a teaching opportunity
- Maintains a personal learning system — bookmarks, notes, a reading list — to stay current without being overwhelmed
- Contributes to technical discussions on Hacker News, Reddit (r/programming, r/golang, etc.), and Twitter/X
- Experiments with new technologies in side projects before evaluating them for production adoption
- Blocks deep-work time and guards it from interruptions; context switching is expensive and avoided intentionally

## Language

Precise, evidence-based, and occasionally opinionated. Comfortable with deep technical vocabulary (eventual consistency, cyclomatic complexity, observability, ADR, idempotency, backpressure) but allergic to buzzwords that lack engineering substance. Values clarity over cleverness in both code and communication. Dry humor is appreciated. Responds better to "here is why this works" than "here is what to do." Skeptical of hype cycles until proven in production.

## Decision Drivers

- Engineering correctness — the right approach over the convenient one, when the tradeoff is worth paying
- Maintainability — will future developers (including future-me) understand and safely change this?
- Performance evidence — benchmarks, profiling data, and real-world measurements over theoretical claims
- Team consensus — prefers decisions made through RFC or architectural review rather than unilateral calls
- Tool maturity — evaluates stability, community health, and production track record before adopting dependencies
- Reversibility and testability — avoids tight coupling and opaque abstractions that make systems hard to test or change

## Pain Points

- Constant context switching between feature work, code reviews, incidents, and mentoring leaves little time for deep technical work
- Technical debt is acknowledged but never formally prioritized, so it compounds silently until it becomes a crisis
- Product timelines get set before engineering is consulted, and the resulting shortcuts become the next six months of firefighting
- Knowledge is siloed — the same questions get asked repeatedly because documentation is out of date or nonexistent
- Career ladders at many companies do not have a credible individual-contributor track above senior, forcing unwanted management pivots
- The loudest voice in architectural debates often wins, not the most rigorous argument
