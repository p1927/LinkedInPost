# Narrative Structures

## Overview

A narrative structure is the backbone of a story — the sequence of beats that carries the reader from opening tension to resolution. Choosing the right structure before writing prevents meandering and ensures the post lands with intended emotional impact.

---

## hero-journey

**What it is:** The protagonist faces a challenge, is transformed by the struggle, and emerges with hard-won wisdom to share.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **Ordinary World** — Establish the protagonist's status quo and the inciting challenge
2. **Ordeal** — The struggle, the failure, or the pivotal moment of transformation
3. **Return with Insight** — What the journey taught them, reframed as a lesson for the reader

**Best For:** Personal growth stories, career pivot posts, overcoming failure narratives, mentorship content

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when the protagonist (author or subject) has genuinely changed as a result of the experience
- Avoid when the story is primarily about an external event with no personal transformation
- Strong signal: phrases like "I used to think…", "That changed when…", "Now I know…"

---

## before-after

**What it is:** Contrast two states — a difficult before and a better after — to make the transformation visceral and credible.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **Before** — Paint the painful, frustrating, or limited state in specific, sensory detail
2. **The Shift** — Name the decision, tool, insight, or event that created the change
3. **After** — Show the concrete improvement; quantify if possible

**Best For:** Product demonstrations, process improvements, skill development posts, outcome-focused content

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when you can name a specific, concrete change that caused the transformation
- Works best when the "after" state is measurable or observable
- Avoid if the transformation is vague or if there is no clear causal agent

---

## problem-agitate-solve

**What it is:** Name a problem the audience recognizes, amplify the pain so it feels urgent, then offer a credible solution.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **Problem** — State the specific pain point your audience experiences
2. **Agitate** — Deepen the wound; show the downstream consequences of ignoring it
3. **Solve** — Present the solution as the natural, credible answer

**Best For:** Thought leadership, product positioning, educational content, call-to-action posts

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when the audience already suspects the problem but hasn't fully felt its weight
- Agitation must feel like empathy, not manipulation — name real consequences, not fabricated fears
- Pair with proof (data, case study, testimonial) to make the solve credible

---

## in-medias-res

**What it is:** Drop the reader into the middle of the action — the crisis, the decision, the turning point — then pull back to provide context.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **In the Middle** — Open with the moment of highest tension or consequence
2. **Context** — Rewind to explain who, what, and why this moment matters
3. **Resolution** — Bring the reader back to the present and land the lesson

**Best For:** Attention-grabbing hooks, high-stakes narratives, posts competing in noisy feeds

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when the story has a genuinely dramatic or surprising moment worth leading with
- Context beat must be brief — two to three sentences maximum before returning to the story
- Avoid for routine or low-stakes content where the opening tension would feel manufactured

---

## countdown

**What it is:** Structure the post as a numbered list that builds anticipation — either descending toward a reveal or ascending toward a climax.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **Frame the Stakes** — Explain why this list matters and what the reader will gain
2. **The Items** — Each item adds tension, insight, or specificity; order is deliberate
3. **The Payoff** — The final item is the most powerful, surprising, or actionable

**Best For:** Tips posts, ranked insights, "things I wish I knew" content, scannable educational content

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when the content naturally decomposes into discrete, comparable units
- The final item must justify the countdown framing — it should feel like a reveal
- Avoid if the items are of roughly equal weight and no natural climax exists

---

## parallel

**What it is:** Tell two stories simultaneously — separated by time, person, or context — whose convergence or contrast delivers the insight.

**3-Beat Version:**
1. **Thread A** — Introduce the first person, situation, or time period
2. **Thread B** — Introduce the contrasting or parallel case
3. **Convergence** — Reveal what the two threads share, how they diverge, or what the juxtaposition teaches

**Best For:** Comparative analysis, generational perspectives, "two paths" narratives, research-backed content

**Selection Rules:**
- Use when the insight emerges from the relationship between two cases rather than from either case alone
- Threads must be genuinely parallel — same question, different answers — or genuinely contrasting
- Avoid if one thread is clearly more interesting than the other; the imbalance will undermine the structure

---

## Structure Selection Rules

| Signal in Brief | Recommended Structure |
|---|---|
| Personal transformation | hero-journey |
| Measurable before/after | before-after |
| Audience has a known pain | problem-agitate-solve |
| Dramatic opening moment | in-medias-res |
| List of discrete insights | countdown |
| Two contrasting cases | parallel |
| Factual, research-backed | before-after or problem-agitate-solve |
| Short-form, high-noise feed | in-medias-res or countdown |
