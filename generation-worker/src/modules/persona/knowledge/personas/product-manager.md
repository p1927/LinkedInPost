# Product Manager

id: product-manager
name: Product Manager

---

## Concerns

- Prioritizing the right features when stakeholders across engineering, design, sales, and leadership all have competing urgent requests
- Shipping fast enough to stay competitive while avoiding feature bloat that degrades product quality
- Maintaining a coherent product vision while being flexible enough to respond to customer and market signals
- Demonstrating product impact in metrics that executives care about, not just output shipped
- Staying close to customers without being captured by the loudest voices in the room
- Managing relationships with engineering and design so that collaboration stays healthy under deadline pressure

## Ambitions

- Owning a product area that grows from zero to something with measurable market impact
- Becoming the kind of PM that engineers and designers actively want to work with
- Developing a reputation for shipping products that customers love and use repeatedly
- Progressing toward a Group PM, Director of Product, or CPO track through demonstrated scope management
- Building a discovery and delivery process that the team trusts and can execute without constant PM involvement
- Developing a personal point of view on product strategy that influences organizational direction

## Current Focus

Sharpening the product discovery process — doing more structured customer research before committing to solutions, writing cleaner specs with explicit success metrics, and reducing the number of features that ship but go unused.

## Habits

- Reviews usage analytics and customer support tickets weekly to detect emerging patterns
- Maintains a prioritized backlog with written rationale for ordering decisions
- Runs structured user interviews and synthesizes findings into shareable insight documents
- Reads widely across product, business, and design (Lenny's Newsletter, Reforge, Product Hunt launches, competitor blogs)
- Writes PRDs, one-pagers, and strategy memos to align cross-functional stakeholders before committing to build
- Participates in sprint ceremonies and maintains a working relationship with lead engineers on their team

## Language

Frameworks-fluent but outcome-oriented. Comfortable with product terminology (jobs-to-be-done, north star metric, opportunity sizing, discovery vs. delivery, activation funnel) but wary of jargon that masks unclear thinking. Appreciates structured reasoning and concrete examples. Values collaboration language — "we," "the team," "our users" — over individual heroics. Can shift register from technical detail with engineers to executive summary with leadership.

## Decision Drivers

- Customer evidence — data, interviews, and behavioral signals carry more weight than internal opinions
- Strategic alignment — does this fit the product vision and company strategy, or is it a distraction?
- Opportunity cost — what does the team not build if they build this?
- Feasibility and scope — what is the minimal version that tests the core hypothesis?
- Business impact — does this move a metric that leadership and the company genuinely care about?
- Reversibility — can this be rolled back or iterated on quickly if it does not perform?

## Pain Points

- Requirements get misinterpreted between spec and implementation, creating rework at the worst time
- Stakeholders escalate their pet features directly to engineering, bypassing prioritization processes
- It is genuinely hard to know which customer feedback represents a real pattern versus an edge case
- Success metrics are often defined too late or too loosely, making impact measurement murky after launch
- Context-switching between strategic thinking and tactical execution is constant and cognitively expensive
- Getting credit for the right calls is harder than absorbing blame for shipping delays or misses
