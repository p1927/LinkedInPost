# Story Devices

## Overview

Story devices are techniques applied within a narrative structure to sharpen impact, create emotional texture, and improve memorability. Structures define the shape of a story; devices define its texture. A post can use multiple devices simultaneously.

---

## anecdote

**What it is:** A brief, specific real incident that illustrates a larger point — the micro-story inside the macro-argument.

**Tip:** Anchor the anecdote in a specific moment: a date, a conversation, a room. Specificity is what separates an anecdote from a vague claim.

**Example:** "In January 2023, I missed a $40,000 deal because I forgot to follow up once. That morning I built a two-step system I still use today."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use when the abstract point needs grounding in lived reality
- Keep to three sentences or fewer — an anecdote that takes over becomes a digression
- Must be verifiable or at least plausible; invented anecdotes erode trust

---

## metaphor

**What it is:** Describe one thing in terms of another to make an abstract concept immediately tangible.

**Tip:** Choose metaphors from the audience's domain. A sales metaphor lands differently for engineers than it does for marketers.

**Example:** "Building a content strategy without a persona is like optimizing a route before choosing a destination."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use when explaining a concept the audience has no prior frame for
- The source domain (the thing being compared to) must be more familiar than the target domain
- Avoid mixing metaphors — one extended metaphor per post maximum

---

## contrast

**What it is:** Place two opposing ideas, behaviors, or outcomes side by side to make each more vivid.

**Tip:** Contrast works best when the gap between the two sides is surprising. If the reader predicts the contrast, it adds no value.

**Example:** "Most people optimize for speed. The best operators optimize for repeatability."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use when you want to position a counterintuitive insight against a common assumption
- The contrast must be real — not a strawman that nobody actually believes
- Works well as a standalone opening line or as the pivot beat in before-after structure

---

## callback

**What it is:** Return to an image, phrase, or idea introduced earlier in the post, giving it new meaning in light of what has since been revealed.

**Tip:** Plant the callback element in the opening, then activate it in the final beat. The reader's recognition creates a feeling of closure and craft.

**Example:** Opening: "I spent three years chasing speed." Closing: "Speed wasn't the problem. I was."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use when the post is long enough to support a planted element (300+ characters)
- The callback must add new meaning, not merely repeat — recontextualization is the mechanism
- Avoid in short-form posts where the callback distance is too small to feel earned

---

## analogy

**What it is:** Explain how something unfamiliar works by showing how it parallels something the audience already understands deeply.

**Tip:** An analogy is more extended than a metaphor — it maps multiple corresponding elements. Commit to the mapping; partial analogies confuse.

**Example:** "Hiring a great PM is like casting a film director: skills matter less than their ability to hold a vision while managing a hundred competing agendas."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use for educational content where the mechanism of a new idea needs unpacking
- Test the analogy: does it hold at more than one level of detail? If not, use a metaphor instead
- Name the analogy explicitly when the audience is unfamiliar with the source domain

---

## cliffhanger

**What it is:** Withhold a key piece of information until the reader has committed to reading further — creating forward momentum through unresolved tension.

**Tip:** The cliffhanger must be genuine. A vague teaser ("and what happened next changed everything") with a weak payoff destroys trust.

**Example:** "I sent the email at 11pm. By 9am the next morning, I had lost a client, a partner, and almost my job. Here's what I learned."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use as an opening hook when the story has a genuinely dramatic turning point
- The payoff must justify the tension — underdelivering on a cliffhanger is worse than not using one
- Best suited for in-medias-res and hero-journey structures

---

## specific-detail

**What it is:** Replace vague language with precise, concrete detail — numbers, names, timestamps, quantities — to make claims feel real and earned.

**Tip:** One specific detail does more work than five general ones. "Revenue grew" is forgettable. "$2.3M ARR in 14 months" is not.

**Example:** "We cut onboarding time from 11 days to 3 days by removing one approval step that nobody owned."

**Device Selection Rules:**
- Use wherever the draft contains words like "many," "some," "significantly," "improved," or "fast"
- Specifics that feel oddly precise (e.g., "$2,347") are more credible than round numbers
- If a real number is unavailable, use a relative benchmark: "3x faster," "half the cost"

---

## Device Selection Rules

| Goal | Recommended Device |
|---|---|
| Ground an abstract point | anecdote or specific-detail |
| Explain an unfamiliar concept | metaphor or analogy |
| Position a counterintuitive idea | contrast |
| Create a satisfying ending | callback |
| Maximize hook engagement | cliffhanger |
| Add credibility to a claim | specific-detail |
| Short educational post | analogy or metaphor |
| Long narrative post | callback + anecdote |
