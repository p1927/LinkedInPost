# SUCCESs Framework — "Made to Stick" Scoring Guide

The SUCCESs framework (Chip Heath & Dan Heath) identifies six qualities that make ideas memorable and spreadable. Each dimension can be scored 1–10 to evaluate content stickiness before publishing.

---

## Dimension 1: Simple

**Definition:** The core idea is stripped to its single most essential insight. Complexity is removed until one sentence could capture the message.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | One sharp, memorable sentence captures the entire message. A 10-year-old could repeat it accurately. |
| 5 | The main idea is present but competes with 2–3 secondary points. Readers can identify the message but cannot quote it. |
| 1 | Multiple competing ideas. No single takeaway emerges. Readers finish without knowing what to remember. |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** Presence of a single governing idea; supporting points all serve that one idea; no detours into adjacent topics.

---

## Dimension 2: Unexpected

**Definition:** The content violates a schema — it delivers something the reader did not predict. Surprise opens attention; interest sustains it.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | The opening breaks a strong expectation. The reader's prediction is wrong within the first two sentences. Curiosity gap is maintained through the body. |
| 5 | Mildly interesting framing but the conclusion is predictable from the headline. No genuine schema violation. |
| 1 | Fully predictable from title to close. No moment where the reader thinks "I didn't expect that." |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** A counterintuitive opening claim; a statistic or fact that contradicts the obvious assumption; a story whose outcome is genuinely surprising.

---

## Dimension 3: Concrete

**Definition:** The idea is expressed in sensory, specific terms — actions people can visualize, numbers they can picture, examples they can touch.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | Every key point is anchored in a vivid specific: named person, precise number, physical action, real scenario. No abstractions without illustrations. |
| 5 | Some concrete examples present but key points rely on vague nouns ("strategy", "success", "impact") without grounding. |
| 1 | Entirely abstract. High proportion of buzzwords and generalities. Nothing is picturable. |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** Named individuals or companies; specific numbers (not "many" but "73%"); described actions (not "improved" but "cut response time from 4 hours to 11 minutes").

---

## Dimension 4: Credible

**Definition:** The reader has reason to believe the claim — via data, authority, personal experience, or vivid detail that only someone who was there would know.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | Claim supported by: verified statistic with source, authoritative testimony, first-person experience with specific detail, or a "sinatra test" example (if it works here, it works anywhere). |
| 5 | Claim is plausible but unsupported. Reader has no way to evaluate truth. |
| 1 | Claim is either inherently incredible or directly contradicts widely known facts with no supporting evidence. |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** Cited statistics; named sources; specific personal credentials or track record; anti-credentialing (admitting limitations builds trust).

---

## Dimension 5: Emotional

**Definition:** The content makes the reader care about the people or stakes involved. Emotional connection activates the motivation to act or share.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | Reader feels something identifiable: pride, empathy, fear, inspiration, indignation, or hope. Emotional arc is intentional and earned. |
| 5 | Mild positive or negative valence but no specific emotion is evoked. Functionally informative but not moving. |
| 1 | Purely informational. No human stakes, no protagonist, no tension. Reader has no emotional reason to engage further. |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** A named protagonist with a problem; stakes that connect to the reader's identity or aspirations; language of feeling ("felt", "feared", "hoped"); before/after emotional contrast.

---

## Dimension 6: Stories

**Definition:** The idea is delivered through or alongside a narrative: a protagonist facing a challenge, a turning point, and a resolution that carries the lesson.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 10 | Full story arc present: character, conflict, resolution. The insight emerges from the story rather than being stated separately. |
| 5 | Story elements present (character or scenario) but arc is incomplete. Lesson is stated explicitly rather than illustrated. |
| 1 | No narrative. Pure list, abstract argument, or data presentation with no human story. |

**Scoring signals for 8+:** Named protagonist (even "I"); clear before-state and after-state; a moment of decision or discovery; the lesson is shown, not just told.

---

## Composite Scoring Guide

**Total score** = sum of all six dimensions (max 60).

| Total | Stickiness Level | Recommendation |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| 50–60 | Exceptional | Publish as-is; high recall and shareability expected |
| 40–49 | Strong | Minor refinement to weakest dimension(s) |
| 30–39 | Adequate | Strengthen bottom 2 dimensions before publishing |
| 20–29 | Weak | Significant revision needed; revisit core message |
| Under 20 | Poor | Reconsider the brief; fundamental clarity issues |

**Weakest dimension**: The single lowest-scoring dimension. Improvement here yields the highest return on revision effort.

**Improvement hint strategy**: For Simple — cut; For Unexpected — add a counterintuitive opener; For Concrete — replace abstract nouns with specific examples; For Credible — add a data point or personal proof; For Emotional — add a protagonist and stakes; For Stories — wrap the insight in a 3-beat narrative.
